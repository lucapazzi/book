Part IV — Designing Correct Controllers A Correct-by-Construction Methodology

Parts II and III assembled the semantic machinery: an Assembly of interacting components, a controller whose every state carries a specified semantics Sem(s), and an inductively computed obtained semantics Conf(s). What remains is the discipline that turns this machinery into a method—the set of local conditions a controller must satisfy, re-checked continuously as it is built, so that correctness is maintained by construction rather than established after the fact. This part states those conditions. After a compact recap it introduces the design cycle, shows how the reactive space of a state is computed, and then presents the Correct-by-Construction rules that PWSEditor enforces, closing with the notion of a well-formed controller.

1. Where we stand: two semantics and two duties

The material of Parts II–III can be compressed into two ideas, both of which this part now puts to work. Repetita iuvant: the recap is deliberately brief, because everything here was already established—what changes is that we are about to treat it as an operational discipline rather than a description.

Two semantics per state. Every controller state s is associated with two sets of Assembly configurations. The specified semantics Sem(s) is declared by the designer: the constraint domain the state is allowed to represent. The obtained semantics Conf(s) is computed from the controller structure by the inductive construction of Part III: the configurations actually reachable while the controller sits in s. Verification compares the two, and the safety-relevant direction of that comparison is the inclusion

Conf(s) ⊆ Sem(s)

which says the system never reaches a configuration the state was not allowed to admit. When Sem(s) is chosen to exclude the dangerous configurations, this inclusion is a safety property.

Two duties for the controller. A controller must simultaneously drive—use event-triggered transitions with actions to push the Assembly toward its intended configurations—and react—use guard-triggered transitions to answer autonomous evolution that would otherwise carry the Assembly out of Sem(s). A controller that only drives is unsafe under autonomous change; one that only reacts never makes progress. Part III, Figure 5.1 showed the reactive case as two successive moves between possible worlds: a spontaneous move out of the semantics, followed by an induced guard-triggered move that restores consistency.

Because the same objects appear in the theory (Part III), in the rules below, and in the PWSEditor dashboards (Part VI) under slightly different names, it is worth fixing the correspondence once:

Concept This book PWSEditor Origin Meaning
Specified semantics Sem(s) CS declared by the designer constraint domain the state may represent
Obtained semantics Conf(s) SS / Acc computed (inductive fixed point) configurations actually reachable in s
Reactive space RS(s) RS / exit zones computed autonomous successors that leave Sem(s)

The third row—the reactive space—was introduced in Part III and its treatment deferred to here. Section 3 makes it precise; the rules of Section 4 then rest on it.

2. The design cycle

Correct-by-Construction design is an iterative loop in which each step has an immediate, observable semantic consequence. The designer never leaves the loop with an unexplained inconsistency: every edit is followed by a recomputation, and every violated condition is surfaced at once.

  1. Specify. Declare Sem(s) for each controller state—the constraint domain it must represent—before wiring the transitions that reach it.
  2. Construct. Introduce transitions: driving transitions (event-triggered, carrying actions) that advance the Assembly, and reactive transitions (guard-triggered) that answer autonomous evolution.
  3. Compute. Recompute the obtained semantics Conf(s) and the reactive space RS(s) from the current structure, as the least fixed point of Part III.
  4. Compare. Check Conf(s) against Sem(s), and inspect RS(s): which autonomous successors leave the declared domain and thus demand a reaction.
  5. Repair. Cover the exposed exit zones with reactive transitions; correct guards and actions that no longer match the semantics.
  6. Verify. Confirm the local obligations of Section 4 hold on every state.
  7. Repeat until Conf(s) and Sem(s) coincide on every state and no obligation is outstanding.

The contrast with the classical design → then verify workflow is not merely one of ordering. Because each condition is local—it constrains a single state or a single transition—it can be re-evaluated after every edit. In PWSEditor this recomputation is continuous (recalculateSemantics() runs after each change), so correctness is not a phase but a property held invariant throughout construction. Review then focuses on the explicit list of unmet obligations rather than on ad-hoc inspection.

The loop is monotone in the sense of Part III: computing Conf and RS always terminates at a fixed point. What is not automatic is that the fixed point satisfies the obligations—that is the designer's work, and the subject of the next two sections.

3. Computing the reactive space

Driving transitions are chosen by the designer; autonomous transitions are not. Sensors flip, doors swing, timers expire—these are component transitions the controller does not command, yet they move the Assembly from one configuration to another while the controller remains in the same state. The reactive space collects exactly those autonomous moves that matter.

Let s be a controller state with obtained semantics Conf(s), and let ⤳ denote a single autonomous component transition. The reactive space of s is

RS(s) = { c′ | c ∈ Conf(s), c ⤳ c′, c′ ∉ Sem(s) }

that is, the set of configurations reachable by one autonomous step from a currently reachable, in-domain configuration, and landing outside the declared constraints. Each such c′ is an exit zone of s.

The condition c′ ∉ Sem(s) is what makes an exit zone relevant. An autonomous step whose target still satisfies Sem(s) is harmless drift: the state already admits it, and no reaction is required. An autonomous step that leaves Sem(s) breaks the correspondence between the controller state and the Assembly, and must be answered. PWSEditor draws this distinction visually, treating drift that stays within the reachable internal configurations as an internal (grey) exit zone that carries no obligation, and reserving red for the external exit zones that do.

An algorithm for RS(s)

The definition is already an algorithm. To obtain RS(s), enumerate the reachable configurations of the state, push each of them through every autonomous transition available to it, and keep the successors that violate the state's constraints. In pseudocode:

computeReactiveSpace(s):
    RS ← ∅
    for each c in Conf(s):                  # reachable, in-domain configurations
        for each autonomous transition τ enabled at c:
            c′ ← apply(τ, c)                # spontaneous successor
            if c′ ∉ Sem(s):                 # τ left the declared domain
                RS ← RS ∪ { c′ }            # c′ is an (external) exit zone
    return RS

The procedure is effectively computable, and cheaply so. Conf(s) is finite—it is the least fixed point computed in Part III over the finite lattice of Assembly configurations; each component exposes finitely many autonomous transitions, so the inner loop is finite; and the test c′ ∉ Sem(s) is a single constraint evaluation. The two nested loops therefore terminate, and RS(s) is finite, computed in O(|Conf(s)| · d) constraint checks, where d bounds the autonomous out-degree of a configuration. PWSEditor performs exactly this computation for every state inside recalculateSemantics(), refining it by reclassifying successors that remain internally reachable as grey internal exit zones.

Example: Cover–Laser. Take the state Work with Sem(Work) = {(Closed, On)}. The only autonomous transition available at (Closed, On) is the cover opening, cover: Closed ⤳ Open, which yields (Open, On). Since (Open, On) ∉ Sem(Work), this configuration is an exit zone: RS(Work) = {(Open, On)}. It is precisely the spontaneous move of Part III, Figure 5.1.

An exit zone is thus not an error but an obligation: a boundary condition the controller is required to answer. Figure 3.1 shows what discharging it looks like—the same state before and after a covering reaction is added.

4. The Correct-by-Construction rules

The obligations below are local correctness conditions: each concerns a single state or a single transition, and each can be decided after every edit from the recomputed semantics. They are stated as numbered rules R1–R7, followed by the masking rule R8 that governs fail states. Rules R1–R7—except the finer guard-partition checks—are the conditions PWSEditor aggregates into its Controller Report; the remaining checks are live diagram-level diagnostics.

R1 — Constraint consistency (safety). For every state s, Conf(s) ⊆ Sem(s): every computed configuration satisfies the declared constraints. When Sem(s) excludes the unsafe configurations, a configuration outside Sem(s) is one the state promised to avoid but can reach—so this inclusion is the model's safety obligation. PWSEditor: reported as Constraint violation.

Deep dive → Injecting Semantics · R1

R2 — Exit-zone coverage. Every external exit zone of s—every element of RS(s)—is covered by at least one enabled autonomous reaction. Autonomous evolution will reach these configurations; without a reaction, controller and Assembly fall out of correspondence with no recovery. PWSEditor: reported as Uncovered Exit Zone (masked on fail states, see R8).

Deep dive → The Reactive Space · R2

R3 — Reaction determinism. No exit zone enables more than one competing autonomous reaction. Coverage (R2) guarantees a reaction exists; determinism guarantees it is unambiguous. PWSEditor: preserved by the autonomous-guard editor, which hides targets already handled from the same source; not surfaced as a separate report item.

Deep dive → The Reactive Space · R3

R4 — No orphan exit zones. No exit zone refers to a component source state that no longer exists in the Assembly. An orphan exit zone is stale structure left by a model change, and must be removed rather than left uncovered. PWSEditor: reported as Orphan Exit Zone.

Deep dive → The Reactive Space · R4

R5 — Guard well-formedness. Every enabled transition carries a meaningful guard (never the placeholder FALSE); an autonomous guard's target is a currently valid exit zone (not orphan); and, within a group of transitions sharing a source state and trigger, the guards are non-empty on the source semantics, mutually non-overlapping (determinism), and jointly complete (totality). Guards select which configurations a transition transfers (Part III, §4), so a stale, empty, overlapping, or incomplete guard set corrupts that selection. PWSEditor: FALSE Guard and Orphan Guard are reported; the partition checks are diagram-level.

Deep dive → Injecting Semantics · R5

R6 — Action fireability. Every action names a machine.event that is actually fireable from the semantics reaching the transition. An action that is not fireable is a command the component cannot obey. PWSEditor: reported as Orphan Action.

Deep dive → Injecting Semantics · R6

R7 — Reachability and deadlock-freedom (liveness). No state has empty Conf(s) (no unreachable state); every configuration retains an escape path to some enabled outgoing transition, directly or after internal evolution (no primary deadlock); and it is not additionally stuck internally (no secondary deadlock). PWSEditor: reported as Unreachable States, Primary and Secondary Deadlock (deadlocks masked on fail states, see R8).

Deep dive → Keeping the Controller Alive · R7

R8 — Fail-state masking (exception). A state explicitly marked fail—a deliberate fail-safe sink—is exempted from the reactive obligations: R2 (uncovered exit zone) and the deadlock clauses of R7 are masked. Consistency (R1), orphan exit zones (R4), guard and action well-formedness (R5, R6) and reachability (R7, unreachable) remain in force. A fail-safe refuge should not be required to react onward or to avoid "deadlock", but it must still be internally consistent and reachable.

5. Well-formed controllers

A controller is well-formed when every state satisfies rules R1–R7, with R8 relaxing the reactive obligations exactly on the states explicitly marked as fail. This is the condition under which PWSEditor's report reads CONTROLLER IS WELL-FORMED.

Well-formedness is not decoration added to a finished design; it is the precondition under which the semantic analysis is meaningful at all. The obtained semantics, the reactive space, and the comparison with the specified semantics are only trustworthy once the rules hold: a controller that violates them may still execute, but its semantics cannot be analysed by this method—only syntactic checks would remain, and the tool's guarantees would collapse to those.

Two properties make the notion practical. It is local: every rule constrains a single state or transition, so well-formedness decomposes into independently checkable conditions. And it is incremental: because those conditions are re-evaluated after every edit, the global property is held throughout construction rather than proved at the end. This is precisely what "correct by construction" means operationally—correctness is an invariant of the editing process, not a verdict pronounced over a completed artifact.

Well-formedness is also the hinge to Part V. Once a controller together with its directly controlled Assembly is well-formed, it can be abstracted into a component with a contract, and the same discipline applies one level up. Local well-formedness at every level is what lets correctness compose along the controller hierarchy—the subject of compositional verification.

The Correct-by-Construction methodology thus reframes controller design as the maintenance of a small set of local semantic invariants. By making the specified semantics explicit, computing the obtained semantics and the reactive space after every edit, and holding rules R1–R8 at each step, it replaces after-the-fact testing with a construction whose correctness is established as it is built.
